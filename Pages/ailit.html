Project Overview

ChAI Academy is a self-directed learning platform designed to help university administrative staff develop practical and responsible AI skills. The platform combines AI literacy, workplace applications, data privacy, and EU AI regulations in short, accessible learning modules.

The project was completed by an interdisciplinary team of nine. I led the user research and helped ensure that the platform's direction was grounded in the needs, concerns, and working environments of university staff. This project was part of the AI in Education Master group at the University of Bremen.

The Challenge

University administrative staff are increasingly expected to work with AI, but their experience, confidence, and understanding vary significantly. Staff must also consider data privacy, institutional policies, and the EU AI Act when deciding whether and how to use AI.

Our challenge was to understand what staff actually needed from an AI literacy platform, not only what they needed to learn, but also how that learning could fit into their existing responsibilities and workflows.

Research Questions

What AI knowledge and practical skills do university staff need?
Which learning approaches best support staff with different levels of AI experience?
How can an AI literacy platform support responsible, confident, and workplace-relevant AI use?

Research Approach

We conducted eight semi-structured interviews with administrative staff from different areas of the University of Bremen. Participants included staff working in IT, information security, international programs, project management, administration, and professional development.

The interviews lasted approximately 30-45 minutes and explored:

  • Existing knowledge and use of AI
  • Attitudes, confidence, and concerns surrounding AI
  • Workplace tasks that could benefit from AI
  • Preferred learning formats
  • Motivation and engagement
  • Data privacy and ethical concerns
  • Institutional and time-related constraints

Analyzing the Research

The interview data was examined using a combination of deductive and inductive thematic analysis. Deductive analysis allowed us to compare participants' experiences with existing theories of AI literacy, learning, and self-directed education.

Inductive analysis allowed additional needs, behaviors, and concerns to emerge directly from the interviews. This process helped us move beyond assumptions and identify what university employees would actually need from the platform.

Research Outcome

The research directly shaped the platform's structure, content, and feature priorities. It led the team toward a practical microlearning experience centered on realistic administrative tasks rather than a general or highly technical AI course.

The final prototype included:

  • Modular lessons
  • Practical AI exercises
  • Short knowledge checks
  • Progress tracking
  • Lightweight, noncompetitive gamification
  • Role-based functionality
  • Content addressing ethics, privacy, and regulation

Reflection

Leading the research showed me how strongly organizational context influences technology adoption. The primary barrier was not simply a lack of technical knowledge. Participants also needed confidence, practical relevance, clear institutional guidance, and reassurance about responsible use.

Working within an interdisciplinary team also required me to communicate qualitative findings in a way that designers and developers could apply. Turning interview data into clear product requirements was one of my most important contributions to the project.
